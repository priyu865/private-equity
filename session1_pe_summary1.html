Topic 01
The GP / LP Structure
LP — Limited Partners

The capital providers. Typically pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments, insurance companies, and HNWIs. Called "limited" because their liability is capped at their committed capital. They do not manage the fund.

Pension Funds Endowments Sovereign Wealth Insurance Cos.
GP — General Partners

The fund managers. They source deals, deploy capital, manage portfolio companies, and execute exits. Typically contribute only 1–2% of fund capital ("skin in the game") — enough to align incentives with LPs.

Deal Sourcing Portfolio Mgmt Exits
Why the 1–2% GP contribution matters

Without personal capital at risk, GPs could take reckless bets with other people's money. The GP contribution aligns incentives — when the fund loses, the GP loses too. This is the cornerstone of the PE principal-agent relationship.

Topic 02
Fund Economics — "2 and 20"
2%
Management Fee
Charged annually on committed capital (not just deployed). Covers GP salaries, operations, deal sourcing. Paid regardless of performance. On a $1B fund, that's $20M/year just to keep the lights on.
20%
Carried Interest ("Carry")
The GP's share of profits above the hurdle rate (typically ~8%). If LPs haven't received their 8% minimum return first, the GP earns zero carry.
The Hurdle Rate — key nuance

Unlike hedge funds where carry can kick in at any gain, PE carry only activates after LPs receive a preferred return (the hurdle, typically 8%). This ensures GPs are only rewarded for genuinely strong performance.

Topic 03
Fund Selection — The LP Perspective

When an LP commits capital to a PE fund, they're locking it up for ~10 years. Due diligence centres on three evaluation buckets:

1. Track Record

Historical returns (IRR, MOIC), consistency across vintages, performance in downturns. Caveat: track record is backward-looking — past performance doesn't guarantee future returns.

2. Team

Stability and depth of the investment team, prior experience, key-man risk, culture, and succession planning.

3. Strategy

Is their focus (sector, geography, deal size) clearly defined? Is the market opportunity still valid? Does their value creation playbook hold up given current market dynamics?

⚠ Continuation Vehicles (CVs) — The Conflict of Interest

When a GP moves a portfolio company into a CV rather than exiting, they set the valuation themselves — without an external buyer. A high valuation benefits Fund I LPs (and the GP's carry) at the expense of new CV investors buying in at an inflated price. Regulators increasingly require independent fairness opinions on CV transactions.

Topic 04
PE Investment Strategies
Leveraged Buyout (LBO)

The most common PE strategy. Acquire a mature, cash-generative business using significant debt financing. Value is created through operational improvements, debt paydown, and multiple expansion at exit.

Growth Equity

Investment in established, profitable businesses seeking capital to accelerate growth. Less leverage than LBOs, more focus on revenue expansion.

Distressed / Turnaround

Investing in companies facing financial or operational distress — often buying debt or equity at a deep discount. Valuation is based on projected post-turnaround cash flows.

Take-Private

Acquiring a publicly listed company and delisting it. Thesis: public markets undervalue the business, or it needs restructuring away from quarterly earnings pressure.

Buy-and-Build (Platform + Bolt-ons)

Acquire a "platform" company then add smaller bolt-on acquisitions to create a larger, more valuable entity through synergies. Common in fragmented industries.

Topic 05
The Three Valuation Methods

PE firms use all three in combination. If they converge, confidence is high. If they diverge, something needs explaining.

Method A
DCF

Values the business based on the present value of future cash flows. The most fundamentally rigorous approach.

Project future free cash flows → choose a discount rate (WACC) → discount back → add terminal value.

Value = Σ CF_t / (1+r)^t + TV
Best for: stable businesses with predictable CFs. Also used post-turnaround in distressed situations.
Method B
LBO

Works backwards from a target return. "Given my required IRR and expected exit, what's the maximum I can pay today?"

Target exit value in 5 years → discount back at required return → entry price ceiling.

Entry = Exit Value / (1.20)^5
Best for: buyouts where return targets and debt capacity drive price discipline.
Method C
Multiples

Values the business based on what the market pays for comparable companies. Fast, market-anchored, widely used in negotiations.

Find comparable transactions → apply their EV/EBITDA multiple to your target.

Value = EBITDA × Multiple
Best for: quick sanity checks, negotiation anchoring, and data-rich sectors.
The Key Distinction

DCF asks "what is it worth?" — LBO asks "what can I pay?" — Multiples asks "what is the market paying for similar assets?" Three lenses, one conclusion.

Topic 06
The PE Fund Lifecycle
01
Fundraising
GP pitches to LPs. Capital committed, not yet deployed.
02
Investment
Deal sourcing, due diligence, acquisitions. Years 1–5.
03
Value Creation
Operational improvements, add-ons, debt paydown.
04
Exit
IPO, M&A, secondary buyout, or CV.
05
Wind Down
Fund liquidated. Carry distributed after hurdle met.
Illiquidity Premium

A typical PE fund runs for 10 years. This illiquidity is why LPs demand higher returns than liquid public market alternatives — compensation for locking up capital.

Topic 07
Industry Context — 2022–2024
01
Rising rates killed deal activity
LBOs depend on cheap debt. When rates spiked in 2022–23, debt became expensive → fewer deals → fundraising slowed. PE is structurally rate-sensitive.
02
The exit problem
Volatile public markets shut the IPO window. GPs holding companies they couldn't exit → couldn't return capital to LPs → LPs reluctant to commit to new funds.
03
The denominator effect
As public assets fell in value, PE became over-weight in LP portfolios. Many LPs structurally unable to commit to new PE funds even if they wanted to.
04
CV and secondaries boom
Unable to exit via traditional routes, GPs turned to CVs and secondary sales — raising the conflict-of-interest concerns covered in Topic 03.
💬 Class-ready opinion

"The PE industry is rate-sensitive by nature — the 2022–2023 slowdown was a structural response to monetary tightening, not a fundamental problem with the asset class. As rates normalize, deal activity, exits, and fundraising should recover — but firms that managed their portfolios well through the downturn will emerge with a significant competitive advantage."

Quick Reference
Key Terms to Know Cold
IRR

Internal Rate of Return. The annualised return of an investment. PE typically targets 20%+ IRR. Time-sensitive — the faster you return capital, the higher the IRR.

MOIC

Multiple on Invested Capital. How many times you got your money back. 3x MOIC = $1 invested returned $3. Complements IRR — a 3x over 10 years is far less impressive than 3x over 3 years.

Hurdle Rate

The minimum return LPs must receive before GPs earn carry. Typically 8%. Ensures GPs are rewarded only for genuine outperformance.

Vintage Year

The year a PE fund made its first investment. Used to compare fund performance against peers raised in the same market environment.

EV / EBITDA

Enterprise Value to EBITDA. The most common PE valuation multiple. Strips out capital structure and tax differences, enabling clean cross-company comparison.

Dry Powder

Capital committed by LPs but not yet deployed. High dry powder signals the industry has money to invest but is waiting for the right opportunity or pricing.
